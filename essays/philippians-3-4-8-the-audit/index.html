Philippians 3:4-8 turns on one verb: to count

The Audit: Philippians 3:4-8

Todd Townsend · July 2026

Philippians 3:4-8 turns on one verb. Hēgeomai: to count, to reckon, to render a considered verdict. It is bookkeeping language, and Paul uses it three times in two verses: these things I have counted loss; I count all things loss; I count them refuse.

The verb is borrowed. One chapter earlier, Paul told the Philippians to adopt a certain mind: "in humility counting (hēgoumenoi) one another as surpassing (hyperechontas) yourselves" (2:3). Then he showed them where that mind comes from: "who, being in the form of God, did not count (hēgēsato) equality with God a thing to be seized" (2:6). Hēgeomai occurs six times in this short letter. Five of the six are acts of valuation, and the hymn holds the decisive one.

A second word travels with it. Hyperechō, "to surpass," appears exactly three times in Philippians: the command of 2:3, the peace of 4:7, and here, "the surpassing worth (to hyperechon) of the knowledge of Christ Jesus my Lord" (3:8). Only in 2:3 and 3:7-8 does Paul pair the two words.

So when Paul counts his gains as loss because of a surpassing worth, he is not reaching for fresh vocabulary. He is running his own biography through the grammar of the hymn he has just told the Philippians to adopt as their mind (2:5). That transposition is the passage's load-bearing frame, and the text's other features settle into place around it: the shape of the credential list, the shift of tense between verses 7 and 8, the stacked particles, the crude final noun, the purpose clause left hanging at the unit's edge. What follows works through the passage in its two movements, the inventory (vv. 4-6) and the reversal (vv. 7-8), and then surfaces the hymn underneath.

The frame: verses 1-3

The unit does not start cold. In 3:2 Paul fires three imperatives at one group: "Watch out for the dogs, watch out for the evil workers, watch out for the katatomē." That last word is a coinage. Not peritomē, "circumcision," a cutting around, but katatomē, "mutilation," a cutting down, the verb the Septuagint uses for the self-lacerating prophets of Baal on Carmel (1 Kings 18:28 LXX, katetemnonto). The people insisting that Gentile believers be circumcised are renamed, by a one-letter surgical pun, as pagan self-mutilators. And "dogs," Judaism's stock label for the unclean Gentile outsider, is turned back on them.

Verse 3 gives the positive counterpart, and it defines the key phrase of our passage: "For we are the circumcision, who serve by the Spirit of God and boast (kauchōmenoi) in Christ Jesus and have not put confidence in flesh (ouk en sarki pepoithotes)." "Confidence in flesh," then, is not a general term for sinfulness. It is trust resting in the sphere of pedigree, ritual status, and observable performance: everything the agitators are selling. The participle pepoithotes is perfect tense, confidence placed and still resting there. A settled lean.

One more piece of the frame matters. Philippi was a Roman colony, refounded by Octavian for army veterans and proud of its Italian legal status; its public life was saturated with rank, office, and inscribed honors, and Luke's Philippians introduce themselves with "we are Romans" (Acts 16:21). Paul plays to this register across the letter: "conduct yourselves as citizens" (politeuesthe, 1:27), "our citizenship (politeuma) is in heaven" (3:20). A man standing up in Philippi to recite his credentials, and then write them off, is performing something a colony of status-keepers could feel in the body.

Philippi was a colony of status-keepers—rank you could feel in the body

The inventory: verses 4-6

4 though I myself have grounds for confidence even in flesh.

If anyone else presumes to trust in flesh, I have more:

5 as to circumcision: an eighth-day man;

out of the nation of Israel;

of the tribe of Benjamin;

a Hebrew born of Hebrews;

as measured by law: a Pharisee;

6 as measured by zeal: persecuting the church;

as measured by righteousness, the kind that is in the law: having become blameless.

A few renderings above carry weight in the argument and need to be laid on the table before the reading proceeds.

Load-bearing

  • pepoithēsin ... pepoithenai (v. 4). "Confidence ... to trust," the noun and the perfect infinitive of peithō, picking up the perfect participle of v. 3. What must not be lost is that v. 4 concedes the contest is real. Paul does not say the currency is counterfeit; he says he holds more of it than anyone: egō mallon, "I more," a verbless comparative slap.
  • kata + accusative, three times (vv. 5b-6). "According to," but functionally "as measured by the standard of." Law, zeal, and righteousness are three grids of assessment, and Paul reports his score on each. Translations that vary the preposition ("as to the law ... as for zeal ... as for righteousness," ESV) obscure that the same measuring operation is run three times.
  • genomenos amemptos (v. 6). "Having become blameless": aorist participle of ginomai plus amemptos. The argument depends on what "blameless" measures, on which see below; but note already that ginomai + amemptos is exactly the wording of God's charge to Abraham in the covenant-of-circumcision chapter: "walk pleasingly before me and become blameless (ginou amemptos)" (Genesis 17:1 LXX).

Depth

  • peritomē oktaēmeros (v. 5). A dative of respect ("with respect to circumcision") plus a rare adjective: literally "an eighth-dayer." Circumcision on the eighth day (Genesis 17:12; Leviticus 12:3) means a native-born son of an observant house, as opposed to a proselyte circumcised as an adult or an Ishmaelite circumcised at thirteen. The credential is not "circumcised" but "circumcised on schedule, from birth."
  • ek genous Israēl (v. 5). Genos is birth-stock; "Israel" is the insider covenant name, the name the people used of themselves before God, where Ioudaios was the label used toward outsiders. Compare Paul's parallel escalation in 2 Corinthians 11:22: "Hebrews? So am I. Israelites? So am I. Seed of Abraham? So am I."
  • phylēs Beniamin (v. 5). Benjamin: the only son of Jacob born inside the land (Genesis 35:16-18); the tribe that gave Israel its first king, Paul's namesake Saul (1 Samuel 9; Acts 13:21); the tribe that stayed with Judah and the house of David at the schism (1 Kings 12:21), on whose territory Jerusalem sat. Most diaspora Jews could not name their tribe. Paul can.
  • Hebraios ex Hebraiōn (v. 5). Language and lineage: an Aramaic- and Hebrew-speaking Jew of Aramaic- and Hebrew-speaking parents, the Hebraioi as distinct from the Greek-speaking Hellēnistai (Acts 6:1). Born in Tarsus, yes, but of a diaspora family that never assimilated.
  • diōkōn (v. 6). Present participle, "persecuting." The tense presents the activity as the live content of the credential, not a regretted footnote appended to it. On why persecution belongs in an asset column at all, see below.

Reading the list

The seven items divide four and three. The first four are birthrights: nothing in them was achieved, everything was conferred by parentage. The last three, each introduced by kata, are performances: a chosen party affiliation, a demonstrated zeal, an attained standing. The list covers the whole field of "flesh" as v. 3 defined it, what a man inherits and what he accomplishes, and it is arranged to escalate from cradle to career. It is also bracketed, whether by design or simply because these are Genesis 17's own categories, by the vocabulary of the covenant chapter: eighth-day circumcision (Genesis 17:12) opens it, "become blameless" (Genesis 17:1) closes it. These are, in the most literal sense, Abrahamic covenant credentials.

Abrahamic covenant credentials—what a man inherits and what he accomplishes

Two items sound like liabilities to a modern reader. Both were assets, and the argument depends on hearing them that way.

"As measured by zeal: persecuting the church." In Second Temple Judaism, zeal was not enthusiasm. It was a specific, storied covenant virtue with a violent pedigree, and its founding exemplar is Phinehas, who ran a spear through an Israelite man and a Midianite woman and thereby stopped the plague at Baal-peor. God's verdict was that "he was zealous with my zeal," and the reward was a covenant of perpetual priesthood (Numbers 25:11-13).

Psalm 106:30-31 canonizes the episode with a formula worth pausing over: Phinehas intervened, "and it was reckoned to him as righteousness (kai elogisthē autō eis dikaiosynēn), from generation to generation forever" (Psalm 105:31 LXX). Word for word in the Greek, that is Genesis 15:6, where Abraham's faith is reckoned as righteousness. The Hebrew Bible contains two reckonings of righteousness. One for trusting the promise. One for killing for the covenant.

The tradition ran with the second. When Mattathias kills the apostate Jew at the altar in Modein, the narrator says he "burned with zeal for the law, as Phinehas did against Zimri son of Salu" (1 Maccabees 2:26), and his rallying cry is "let everyone who is zealous for the law and supports the covenant come out with me" (2:27). On his deathbed he tells his sons to "show zeal for the law and give your lives for the covenant of our ancestors" (2:50), and two verses later, in the same breath, recites the other reckoning: "Was not Abraham found faithful when tested, and it was reckoned to him as righteousness?" (2:52). Zeal and reckoned righteousness sit side by side in one economy of covenant credit. Ben Sira ranks Phinehas "third in glory" behind Moses and Aaron for his zeal (Sirach 45:23).

This is the tradition Paul is scoring himself against, and he says so elsewhere in nearly these terms: "I persecuted the church of God violently ... being exceedingly zealous (zēlōtēs) for the traditions of my fathers" (Galatians 1:13-14; compare Acts 22:3-4, where zeal and persecution are again a single item). Luke even gives the credential a face. When the witnesses stoned Stephen, they laid their garments at the feet of a young man named Saul (Acts 7:58). By the tradition's own logic, the coats piling up at his feet were evidence of righteousness.

So "as measured by zeal: persecuting the church" is not a confession smuggled into a résumé. Within the Phinehas tradition it is the résumé's apex, the one entry proving the zeal was not talk. The horror, which Paul makes no move to soften, is that "persecutor of the church" once sat in his asset column, and by that tradition's logic it belonged there.

One discipline note. Philippians itself does not use logizomai, the reckoning verb of Genesis 15:6 and Psalm 106:31; Paul's verb here is hēgeomai, and when logizomai does appear at 3:13 it is doing other work. The Phinehas connection is therefore anchored in the zeal texts themselves, which share the formula verbatim, and in Paul's self-description in Galatians 1. That Paul composed this line to be heard against that tradition is an inference from those anchors. A strong one, but an inference.

"As measured by righteousness, the kind that is in the law: having become blameless." Amemptos does not claim sinlessness. It is the Septuagint's own category for covenant fidelity: God's charge to Abraham (Genesis 17:1), the narrator's verdict on Job (Job 1:1, 8 LXX), and Luke's description of Zechariah and Elizabeth, "walking blamelessly in all the commandments and statutes of the Lord" (Luke 1:6). The law itself contained the machinery for lapse, sacrifice, purification, restitution; a blameless Israelite was one who used it. Paul's claim is that, measured by the Torah's own operational standard, his record was faultless, and nothing in the text hints otherwise.

This is worth stating plainly, because a long Western tradition running from Augustine's reading of Romans 7 through Luther has imagined the pre-Damascus Paul as a man tormented by an accusing conscience, driven to Christ by the law's crushing weight. Verse 6 is the flattest possible contradiction of that picture. The man of verses 4-6 is not failing and miserable. He is succeeding and satisfied. Which means the reversal of verse 7 cannot be explained by anything on the left side of the ledger.

Nothing pushed him out. Something pulled.

The reversal: verses 7-8

7 But whatever things were gains to me, these I have counted, because of the Christ, a loss.

8 More than that: I even count all things to be a loss,

because of the surpassing worth of the knowledge of Christ Jesus my Lord,

because of whom I have forfeited all things,

and I count them refuse, so that I may gain Christ,

Again, the renderings that carry weight:

Load-bearing

  • hēgēmai (v. 7) versus hēgoumai (v. 8, twice). The same verb, first in the perfect tense, then in the present, a shift almost every English translation flattens into an undifferentiated "I count(ed)." The perfect hēgēmai denotes a verdict rendered in the past whose force still stands: the audit happened once and has never been revised. (The only other New Testament occurrence of this perfect is Acts 26:2.) The present hēgoumai then re-runs the verdict as current, ongoing activity. Rendering them "I have counted ... I count" keeps both on the page.
  • kerdē ... zēmian (v. 7). Plural "gains," singular "loss": the many line items of vv. 5-6 re-booked as one undifferentiated write-off. The vocabulary is commercial and legal; zēmia is what a merchant suffers when cargo goes overboard (so Acts 27:10, 21).
  • alla menoun ge kai (v. 8). Four particles stacked at the head of the verse, an escalation English can only approximate: "but more than that: indeed, even." The pile-up signals that v. 8 outbids v. 7, and it does so three ways at once, on which see below.
  • dia, three times (vv. 7-8). "Because of the Christ ... because of the surpassing worth of the knowledge ... because of whom." The cause of the revaluation is stated three times in two verses, and all three times the cause is Christ.
  • ezēmiōthēn (v. 8). Aorist passive of zēmioō: "I was made to forfeit," "I suffered the confiscation of." Where hēgoumai is the ongoing act of valuation, this is the punctiliar event of actual loss, the moment the assets were seized.
  • skybala (v. 8). See below; "rubbish" (ESV, NIV) launders the register.
  • kerdēsō (v. 8). Aorist subjunctive of kerdainō, the cognate verb of kerdē in v. 7: "so that I may gain Christ." The ledger metaphor completes itself.

Depth

  • to hyperechon tēs gnōseōs (v. 8). An articular neuter participle, "the surpassing-ness of the knowledge": worth measured comparatively, above everything on the list. The third strand of the hyperechō thread (2:3; 4:7).
  • tou kyriou mou (v. 8). "My Lord." Paul's letters otherwise confess "our Lord"; the first-person singular possessive appears nowhere else in his correspondence. At the exact point where he strips off every inherited and achieved identity, the replacement identity arrives in the most personal grammar he ever uses for it.
  • gnōseōs Christou Iēsou (v. 8). Objective genitive: the knowing of Christ, relational and covenantal, not a body of information about him. On its scriptural pedigree, see below.

Reading the reversal

Verse 7 renders the verdict. Verse 8 escalates it three ways. Scope: hatina, "whatever things," the specific credentials of vv. 5-6, widens to panta, all things, whatever else might ever be entered on the gains side. Tense: the settled past verdict becomes present, continuous re-affirmation. Valuation: zēmia, "loss," a neutral accounting term, is replaced by skybala, which is not neutral at all.

Skybala occurs only here in the New Testament. Its attested range in the papyri and literary sources runs from table scraps and kitchen refuse through street garbage to excrement; the KJV's "dung" is closer to the register than the modern translations' tidy "rubbish." It is a crude word, chosen to break the polished cadence of the list it condemns. Ancient lexicographers derived it from "what is thrown to the dogs" (es kynas ballein); that etymology is folk linguistics and should not be leaned on, but if the association was live for first-century hearers, the passage closes a loop it opened at v. 2: the man who has just called the agitators "dogs" says his own former treasures are what one scrapes off the plate for them. Hold that connection loosely. The word's coarse register is certain; the canine echo is only suggestive.

Notice, too, where the present tenses are being spoken from. Paul writes in chains (1:7, 13). The forfeiture of ezēmiōthēn is not theoretical: the confiscation happened long ago, the losses of status, network, and standing are decades old, and the man re-running the books in prison finds that the audit still closes the same way.

The three dia phrases carry the theology of the whole unit. Paul never says the credentials were bad. Circumcision, Israel, Benjamin, Torah, even blamelessness under the law: none of these is trashed for what it is. They are re-priced, and the re-pricing has a stated cause, repeated three times so it cannot be missed: because of the Christ; because of the surpassing worth of the knowledge of Christ Jesus my Lord; because of whom. The accounting is christologically generated, not psychologically generated. It is never disappointment with the credentials.

The logic, in other words, is comparative, not contemptuous. It is the merchant's logic, selling everything he has for the one pearl (Matthew 13:45-46), and it is the same accounting Jesus runs in the other direction: what does it profit a man to gain (kerdēsē) the whole world and forfeit (zēmiōthē) his life (Mark 8:36, using precisely this word pair). When the surpassing thing enters the ledger, everything else re-prices automatically. The old assets are not slandered; they are outclassed to the point of write-off, because held onto as grounds of confidence they would now cost him the one acquisition that matters: hina Christon kerdēsō.

Re-priced because of Christ—not contempt for the list

And "the knowledge of Christ Jesus my Lord" is itself a loaded phrase. The one boast Israel's Scriptures permit is exactly this: "let not the wise man boast in his wisdom ... but let him who boasts boast in this, that he understands and knows me, that I am the LORD" (Jeremiah 9:23-24). Paul carries that verse in his active repertoire, quoting it twice ("let him who boasts boast in the Lord," 1 Corinthians 1:31; 2 Corinthians 10:17), and both of its key terms structure this passage: boasting in Christ Jesus (kauchōmenoi, 3:3) over against confidence in flesh, and the surpassing worth of knowing him (3:8), resumed as "to know him" (gnōnai auton, 3:10). Paul does not quote Jeremiah here, so call it a resonance rather than an anchor. But it is a resonance with teeth. In Jeremiah, the object of that knowing is YHWH. Paul transfers the whole formula, boast and knowledge together, to "Christ Jesus my Lord" without comment, as though no transfer were needed.

One grim piece of wordplay belongs to this unit's afterlife in the chapter. The verb of Paul's proudest credential, diōkōn, "persecuting" (3:6), reappears twice within a few verses: "I pursue (diōkō), if somehow I may lay hold" (3:12); "I pursue toward the goal, for the prize of the upward call" (3:14). The same verb, the same intensity, a new object. The zeal was never the problem. The zeal has been re-aimed.

The hymn underneath

Now the deep structure can be stated in full. Philippians 2:6-11 tells the story of one who possessed the highest conceivable status, "being in the form (morphē) of God," and did not count (ouch hēgēsato) that status a thing to be seized and exploited (harpagmon), but emptied himself (heauton ekenōsen), taking the form (morphēn) of a slave, being found (heuretheis) in appearance as a man, and humbled himself (etapeinōsen) to death, even death on a cross; wherefore God super-exalted him and gave him the name at which every knee bows and every tongue confesses that Jesus Christ is Lord (Kyrios). Paul introduces it with a command: "have this mind (phroneite) among yourselves, which is also in Christ Jesus" (2:5).

Chapter 3 is Paul obeying his own command in public, and the verbal anchors are dense enough to exclude coincidence:

  • Christ did not count (hēgēsato) his status exploitable gain (2:6); Paul has counted (hēgēmai) and counts (hēgoumai) his status loss (3:7-8). Same verb, same position in the story.
  • The congregation is to count others surpassing (hēgoumenoi hyperechontas, 2:3); Paul counts all things loss because of the surpassing worth (hēgoumai ... dia to hyperechon, 3:8). The letter's only other pairing of these two words.
  • Christ emptied himself (2:7); Paul was emptied out: "I have forfeited all things" (ezēmiōthēn, 3:8). Different verbs, identical motion: full status voluntarily unloaded.
  • Christ was found (heuretheis) in human form (2:7); Paul's aim is to be found (heurethō) in Christ (3:9), the very next clause after our unit ends.
  • Christ took the form (morphē) of a slave and died (2:7-8); Paul is being conformed (symmorphizomenos) to his death (3:10), and awaits a body conformed (symmorphon) to the body of Christ's glory (3:21).
  • Christ humbled himself (etapeinōsen, 2:8); the believer's present body is "the body of our humiliation" (tapeinōseōs, 3:21).
  • The hymn ends with every tongue confessing Kyrios (2:11); Paul's ledger pivots on "Christ Jesus my Lord" (3:8), and the chapter ends awaiting "the Lord Jesus Christ" from heaven, who will "subject all things to himself" (3:20-21), the cosmic subjection of 2:10 re-sounded.
  • The frame verb recurs too: "have this mind" (phroneite, 2:5) becomes "let those of us who are mature think this way" (phronōmen, 3:15), against the enemies of the cross "whose mind is set on earthly things" (phronountes, 3:19).

The pattern is confirmed by what Paul does next: "join in imitating me, brothers, and watch those who walk according to the pattern (typon) you have in us" (3:17). He can offer himself as the pattern because he has just narrated himself as the worked example of the master pattern. This reading is broadly shared across serious scholarship on the letter; N. T. Wright in particular builds his interpretation of the passage on it, restating Paul's move in the hymn's own vocabulary: the status Paul possessed by birth he refused to treat as something to exploit, and he emptied himself into the Messiah's death so as to receive from God the standing that actually counts. Whatever one makes of Wright's further, contested move on verse 9 (see the note below), the structural observation itself is anchored in the shared Greek, not in any scholar's system.

One caution keeps the parallel honest. It is analogy, not identity. Christ's status was real and infinitely higher, and what he refused to exploit was equality with God; Paul's status was real and covenantal, and what he refused to exploit was privilege within Israel. The hymn's downward arc is unrepeatable. What is repeatable, and commanded (2:5; 3:17), is the counting: the refusal to grip status as leverage, and the trust that God, not the self, writes the exaltation clause. Paul's grammar keeps the two stories distinct at exactly this point. Christ's exaltation is narrated as accomplished, God super-exalted him (2:9); Paul's is subjunctive and hedged: "that I may gain ... that I may be found ... if somehow I may attain the resurrection from the dead" (3:8-11).

Same verb, same position in the story

Where the sentence lands

The unit ends at "so that I may gain Christ," which is not where Paul's sentence ends; the period does not fall until verse 11. The cut is artificial, but it is oddly faithful to the passage, because the audit itself refuses to close. Gaining Christ is immediately glossed as being found in him (3:9), not holding "my own righteousness, the kind from the law", the very standing verse 6 certified as blameless, now declined as a possession, but the righteousness from God on the basis of faith. And the "knowledge" of verse 8 is glossed as knowing him: the power of his resurrection, the fellowship of his sufferings, conformity to his death, "if somehow I may attain the resurrection from the dead" (3:10-11).

The audit refuses to close—mid-story, still pursuing

The audit of verses 7-8, in other words, is not an ascetic exercise in renunciation for its own sake. It is the opening entry of a story with the hymn's shape, and the final entry in the gains column is not written until the body of humiliation is transformed to match the body of glory (3:21). Every verb of acquisition in the sentence stays subjunctive: may gain, may be found, may know, may attain. Paul ends the chapter as he ends the audit, mid-story: forgetting what lies behind, pursuing what lies ahead.
