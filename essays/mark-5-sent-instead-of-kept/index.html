As he was stepping into the boat, the man who had been demonized kept begging him that he might be with him. (Mark 5:18)

Mark 5:18-20: Sent Instead of Kept

Todd Townsend · July 2026

Every other person Jesus heals in the first half of Mark is told to keep quiet. This man is told to talk. He is also the only healed person in the Gospel who asks to join the traveling company and is refused. Those two facts belong together, and they are not arbitrary: Mark has built the closing scene of the Gerasene exorcism as a deliberate photographic negative of the call of the Twelve in 3:14.

The man asks for exactly the thing the Twelve were given, is denied it, and is then handed the other half of their commission. Reading the coda that way is not a homiletical flourish. It rests on a verbatim verbal link that the Greek makes unmistakable and most English translations quietly dissolve.

Here is the unit, broken to keep the three speakers and the shifts between them visible.

18 And as he was stepping into the boat, the man who had been demonized kept begging him that he might be with him.

19 And he did not permit him, but says to him:
"Go to your home, to your own people, and report to them how much the Lord has done for you, and how he had mercy on you."

20 And he went off and began to herald throughout the Decapolis how much Jesus had done for him. And everyone was marveling.

Translation notes

Load-bearing

18, "that he might be with him" (hina met' autou ē). Most versions render this "begged to go with him" (NIV, NLT), which flattens it into a request for a ride. The Greek is a purpose clause built on the verb "to be": that he might be with him. It is the same construction Mark used when Jesus appointed the Twelve in 3:14, hina ōsin met' autou, "that they might be with him." Same preposition, same verb, same syntactic frame. The man is not asking for transport. He is asking, in Mark's own technical vocabulary, to be made a disciple. The whole reading below turns on this being a real echo rather than a coincidence, so it is worth saying plainly why it is real: the phrase "be with him" is not idiomatic Greek for "accompany," and Mark had ordinary verbs available for that; he chose the wording that names the office of the Twelve.

18, "the man who had been demonized" (ho daimonistheis). Not "the demoniac." The participle is aorist passive, a bounded form that presents the demonization as a closed episode: the man who was, at one point, demonized, and now is not. Mark refuses to name him by the condition Jesus has just ended.

19, "the Lord" (ho kyrios) versus 20, "Jesus" (ho Iēsous). This is the hinge of the passage and it is invisible in a translation that reads both as generic. Jesus tells the man to report what the Lord (ho kyrios) has done. The man goes and proclaims what Jesus (ho Iēsous) has done. The subject of the verb changes between the command and its execution, and Mark does nothing to explain the change. See the analysis; the substitution is the point, not a slip.

20, "began to herald" (ērxato kēryssein). Not merely "tell." Kēryssō is the herald's verb, the word for public proclamation. It is what John does (1:4), what Jesus does (1:14, 1:38-39), and the second half of what the Twelve are appointed to do in 3:14 (hina apostellē autous kēryssein, "that he might send them out to herald"). The healed man performs the apostolic verb.

Depth

19, "report" (apangeilon, aorist imperative) is a lower-register word than kēryssein in v. 20. Jesus assigns a household errand ("report to your own people"); the man escalates it into regional heralding. The verbs track the widening scope.

19, "has done ... had mercy" (pepoiēken ... ēleēsen). The tenses differ: perfect for the doing, aorist for the mercy. The perfect frames the Lord's action as an accomplished state that still stands; the aorist points to the decisive act of compassion. In v. 20 the man's report uses the aorist epoiēsen for both, collapsing the pair into a single completed deed done by Jesus.

19, "to your home, to your own people" (eis ton oikon sou pros tous sous). Two phrases, not one: to the house, and to the kin. The man is sent back to the precise society that had chained him among the tombs.

Jesus made twelve… that they might be with him and that he might send them out to herald. (Mark 3:14)

The negative image of the Twelve

To feel the force of v. 18 you have to hear where the reader has already met its wording. In 3:14 Jesus "made twelve, whom he also named apostles, hina ōsin met' autou, that they might be with him and that he might send them out to herald." The appointment has two clauses: presence, then mission. Be with him, then sent to proclaim. Discipleship in Mark is defined by that pairing.

Now the restored man, at v. 18, asks for the first clause in its exact words: hina met' autou ē, that he might be with him. And Jesus refuses. The refusal is blunt, ouk aphēken auton, "he did not permit him," with no softening. But look at what the man receives in its place. He is sent (hypage, "go"), and he ends up doing precisely the second clause of 3:14: he heralds (kēryssein). He is granted the mission and denied the companionship. He becomes the one figure in Mark who holds one half of the apostolic commission without the other, an apostle in the root sense of one sent out, formed not by three years at Jesus' side but by a single act of deliverance.

This is not sentimental. It cuts against the natural expectation that the person most transformed should be the one who stays closest. Mark has spent the pericope training that expectation and then breaking it, and he does it through a single repeated verb. Across the second half of the story, parakaleō, "beg, implore," rings four times, and each time it is a request put to Jesus. The unclean spirits beg not to be driven from the region (5:10). The demons beg to be sent into the pigs (5:12), and Jesus grants it (epetrepsen, "he permitted them," 5:13). The townspeople beg him to leave their territory (5:17), and he grants that too, boarding the boat (5:18). Then the healed man begs to be with him (5:18), and this is the one request Jesus denies. The demons who want relocation get it; the crowd that wants Jesus gone gets its wish; the man who wants to stay is sent away. The verb makes the pattern audible: everyone who wants distance from Jesus is accommodated, and the one who wants nearness is redirected into mission. The denial is the commission.

What the Lord did, what Jesus did

The most demanding move in the passage is the one that survives least well in English. In v. 19 Jesus tells the man to "report to them how much the Lord has done for you, and how he had mercy on you." On Jesus' lips, and to this man's ears, ho kyrios is the natural word for God. The command is a command to give God the glory: go tell your people what God has done. The register is thoroughly Septuagintal. It is the language of Israel's thanksgiving, the summons of the rescued individual to testify before the assembly. Psalm 66:16 (65 LXX) is the type: "Come, hear, all you who fear God, and I will tell what he has done (hosa epoiēsen) for my soul." Mark's hosa ... pepoiēken sits in that idiom. The man is being told to give the testimony a delivered Israelite gives to Yahweh.

Then comes v. 20. "He went off and began to herald throughout the Decapolis how much Jesus had done for him." The man obeys, and in obeying he substitutes. Told to publish what the Lord did, he publishes what Jesus did. Mark does not flag the change, does not have anyone correct it, does not narrate a theological inference. He simply lets the subject of the sentence shift from ho kyrios to ho Iēsous between the instruction and its execution, and leaves the reader holding both. The Lord's deed and Jesus' deed are the same deed. The Lord's mercy and Jesus' mercy are the same mercy.

This is Mark's characteristic method, and it is worth naming what kind of claim it is. The substitution itself is anchored: it is on the page, in the Greek, unmissable once seen. What the substitution implies, that Jesus occupies the place the sentence reserved for the God of Israel, Mark leaves for the reader to draw rather than asserting outright. He works the same way at the very opening of the Gospel, where the kyrios whose way is prepared in the Isaiah and Malachi citation (1:2-3) turns out to be Jesus. Daniel Johansson, in his study of kyrios in Mark, puts the resulting question and its answer sharply: ask who the kyrios of this Gospel is, and the answer is not one or the other but, paradoxically, both God and Jesus, with the exclusive divinity of Israel's God maintained rather than diluted. Richard Hays reads the whole Gospel along this grain, as a narrative that again and again, "almost subliminally," lets Jesus stand where Israel's Scripture had put the Lord. Verse 20 is one of the cleanest instances in Mark: not a title conferred, not a confession spoken, but a delivered man who cannot tell the story of what God did for him except by telling what Jesus did.

The reading holds only as far as the substitution is genuinely Mark's design and not an accident of style, so it is worth being honest about the ceiling. Mark could conceivably intend nothing more than "Jesus is the Lord's agent, so to speak of one is to speak of the other." But the placement is against that minimal reading. Mark has just spent the prologue establishing Jesus as the kyrios of Isaiah's highway, and he will spend the rest of the Gospel letting the demons, not the disciples, be the ones who correctly name Jesus "Son of the Most High God" (5:7). A narrator that deliberate does not stumble into the one substitution that matters most.

Go to your home, to your own people, and report to them how much the Lord has done for you, and how he had mercy on you.

Sent to the ten cities

Two details of scope close the passage, and both point the same direction. First, the errand grows in the telling. Jesus says report (apangeilon) to your household and your own people (eis ton oikon sou pros tous sous). The man heralds (kēryssein) throughout the Decapolis (en tē Dekapolei), the federation of ten Hellenized cities east of the Jordan. A domestic word of testimony becomes a public proclamation across a Gentile region. This is not disobedience; it is the same overspill of the news that Mark records everywhere else in these chapters, where commands to silence are broken by sheer astonishment. Here, uniquely, there is no command to break. This is the one healing in Mark's first half where Jesus authorizes the telling.

That exception is geographic. Everywhere on the Jewish side of the lake, Jesus silences the healed and the confessing (1:44; 3:12; 5:43; 7:36; 8:26; 8:30), guarding against a messiahship read in nationalistic and political terms by his own people before the cross can define it. On the far shore, among Gentiles who carry no such expectation, that danger is not in play, and the testimony is released. The man is left in exactly the territory the reader has been taught to read as unclean, and told to fill it with proclamation of the Lord who is Jesus.

Second, recall who this man was before v. 18. He lived among the tombs (5:3) and among swine (5:11), a Gentile in a land of the dead and the impure. That specific pairing, dwelling in tombs and living among swine, is the portrait Isaiah draws of rebellious humanity under judgment: a people "who sit among the graves and lodge in secret places, who eat swine's flesh" (Isaiah 65:4). The two most striking marks of the man's degradation are precisely Isaiah's two marks of the nation that provokes God to his face. This is resonance rather than citation, since Mark gives no quotation formula, but it is resonance of an unusually exact kind, and it opens onto the payoff. The Isaiah oracle begins: "I was ready to be found by those who did not seek me; I said, 'Here I am, here I am,' to a nation not called by my name" (Isaiah 65:1). That is the theological situation of the Gerasene shore. Jesus crosses the sea to a place no one summoned him, meets the man who embodies Isaiah's tomb-dwelling, swine-eating outsider, and restores him. The man who was the icon of the nation under wrath becomes the first herald in Gentile land of what the Lord, who is Jesus, has done.

He went off and began to herald throughout the Decapolis how much Jesus had done for him. And everyone was marveling.

Mark ends the scene there, with a Gentile proclaiming across ten Gentile cities and everyone marveling, and the Gospel will not return an apostle to this territory for chapters. The composer has planted, before the mission of the Twelve is even launched (6:7), a lone sent man carrying the second clause of their commission into the world beyond Israel. He was not permitted to be with Jesus. He was permitted to be sent. In Mark's account that is not the lesser gift; it is the shape the mission takes once the Lord has crossed to the other side.
